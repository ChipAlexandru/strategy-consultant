# Storyline Patterns

A library of narrative structures for organizing consulting arguments. These are tools, not templates — use the one that fits the logic of the answer, or invent a hybrid. The goal is clarity, not conformity.

## Pattern 1: Recommendation with Pillars
**Best for**: Questions with a clear answer supported by multiple independent reasons.
**Structure**:
- Headline: The recommendation
- Pillar 1: First reason it is right (with evidence)
- Pillar 2: Second reason it is right (with evidence)
- Pillar 3: Third reason it is right (with evidence)
- Risk/counter-argument and mitigation
- Implementation path

**Example headline sequence**:
1. "Client X should acquire Target Y to enter the fast-growing premium segment"
2. "The premium segment is growing 3x faster than mass-market and Target Y holds 25% share"
3. "Target Y's distribution network would take 5+ years and $200M+ to replicate organically"
4. "Acquisition economics are favorable: expected IRR of 15-20% at current valuation range"
5. "The main risk — integration complexity — is manageable with a phased approach"
6. "We recommend a three-phase integration plan over 18 months"

## Pattern 2: Option Comparison
**Best for**: Questions where the client faces a choice between 2-4 distinct paths.
**Structure**:
- Headline: The decision context and recommended option
- Evaluation framework: the 3-5 criteria that matter most
- Option A assessment (against criteria)
- Option B assessment (against criteria)
- Option C assessment (if applicable)
- Why the recommended option wins
- What makes this decision reversible/irreversible

**Example headline sequence**:
1. "Build is preferred over buy or partner for entering the Southeast Asian market"
2. "Three factors matter most: speed to market, capital efficiency, and long-term control"
3. "Partnering is fastest but limits margin capture and creates dependency"
4. "Acquiring a local player costs $150-200M but carries integration risk in an unfamiliar market"
5. "Building organically takes 18-24 months longer but offers full control at lower all-in cost"
6. "The client's strong balance sheet and 3-year time horizon favor the build path"

## Pattern 3: Diagnostic then Prescription
**Best for**: Questions where the root cause is unclear and must be established before recommending action.
**Structure**:
- Headline: The diagnosis (what is actually going on)
- Symptom: What the client sees and is concerned about
- Root cause analysis: Why it is happening (evidence-based)
- Why common explanations are wrong (if applicable)
- Prescription: What to do about it
- Expected impact and timeline

**Example headline sequence**:
1. "Margin decline is driven by mix shift, not operational inefficiency"
2. "Client margins have fallen 200bps over 3 years while competitors have held steady"
3. "Internal analysis blamed rising logistics costs — but logistics costs rose only 0.3% of revenue"
4. "The real driver: the client's fastest-growing channel (online DTC) carries 8 points lower margin than wholesale"
5. "Channel mix has shifted from 20% DTC to 35% DTC in 3 years, mechanically compressing blended margin"
6. "The fix is not cost reduction — it is improving DTC unit economics through [specific actions]"

## Pattern 4: Phased Roadmap
**Best for**: Complex transformations or multi-year strategic shifts where sequencing matters.
**Structure**:
- Headline: The destination and why it matters
- Current state: Where the client is today
- Phase 1: Quick wins / foundation (0-6 months)
- Phase 2: Core transformation (6-18 months)
- Phase 3: Scale and optimize (18-36 months)
- Decision gates between phases
- Total expected impact

## Pattern 5: Risk-Reward Assessment
**Best for**: Go/no-go decisions, investment decisions, or situations with significant uncertainty.
**Structure**:
- Headline: The opportunity and recommended posture
- The upside case: What happens if it works (with probability assessment)
- The downside case: What happens if it fails (with probability assessment)
- Asymmetry analysis: Is the upside/downside skewed favorably?
- Key uncertainties that will resolve over time
- Recommended decision and hedging strategy

## Combining Patterns
Most real storylines borrow from multiple patterns. A diagnostic that leads to a phased roadmap. A recommendation with pillars that includes an option comparison for the implementation approach. Use whatever combination makes the argument clearest.

## Testing Any Storyline
Regardless of pattern, every storyline must pass these tests:
1. **Headline test**: Read only the headlines in order — does a coherent, complete argument emerge?
2. **Action test**: Does the storyline lead to a specific, actionable recommendation?
3. **Counter-argument test**: Is the most obvious objection addressed?
4. **Evidence test**: Is every major claim supported by sourced evidence?
5. **"So what" test**: Does every section connect to the client's decision?
